## Context

Most Razorpay products are charged at standard transaction rates. **Business goal:** one standard rate was simple, but gave payment-ready businesses little reason to choose or deepen their use of Razorpay. **Product goal:** give businesses a clear route: stay on the standard rate, or choose a subscription plan they can manage later.

The final shape of that route was a three-plan choice during onboarding, with Standard always available and no commitment required:

- **Standard: 2%.** Start with the default rate and change later.
- **Plus: 1.9%** *(recommended)*. Lower fees as payment volume begins to grow.
- **Pro: 1.8%.** More value for businesses expecting higher volume.

The subscription opportunity sits just after onboarding, when the business has given us enough context but has not yet formed a regular payment habit. The intended route: understand the business → offer a clear pricing route → help it make a first payment → support regular payment use over time. The existing journey offered one standard rate and no self-serve pricing choice, leaving committed businesses to switch gateways, contact Sales, or postpone the decision.

## Problem

After a registered business completed onboarding, the product still showed one standard rate, even though we had enough context to offer a more relevant next step. In the existing design, only high-value merchants could request customized pricing through Contact Sales; everyone else saw 2% and nothing more.

The opportunity sat in the short window after setup: the product understood the business, but did not yet use that understanding to help it choose how to start. If pricing stayed unclear, merchants could leave before forming a transaction habit.

Mapping the current journey made the gaps explicit:

- **Discover / Sign up:** the business compares providers, creates an account, begins setup.
- **Onboarding:** it shares business details and expects a relevant next step. *Gap: context collected, then unused.*
- **Setup:** it sees only the standard rate before taking payments. *Gap: no plan choice.*
- **Dashboard:** it cannot easily revisit or manage pricing later. *Gap: no lifecycle.*

A composite from merchant calls made the moment concrete. Meet **Arjun**: he has completed onboarding for his online apparel business and is ready to take payments. Before choosing a pricing route, he needs to know what is optional, what fits his expected volume, and whether he can change his mind later. His journey through the decision:

- **Relieved:** setup complete, ready to begin taking payments.
- **Unsure:** "Is 2% my only choice?" One standard rate gives him no way to judge a better route.
- **Comparing:** Standard and a relevant subscription need to appear as clear alternatives.
- **Understanding:** volume cues make the value of each plan easier to judge.
- **Confident:** Standard stays valid alongside optional pricing routes.
- **In control:** he can revisit or manage the choice once real payment use grows.

Four situations kept repeating:

- **Cautious starters:** "I am just starting out. I will upgrade later if needed."
- **Value-conscious comparers:** "The rate is better, but I do not remember the exact calculation."
- **Confused buyers:** "Do I need to pay to start accepting payments?"
- **Setup-blocked merchants:** "I was still stuck on setup, so the plan did not matter yet."

That translated into three shifts the design had to make:

- From **one standard rate for every business** → to **a relevant plan based on business context**.
- From **working out fees and value alone** → to **seeing savings at the expected payment volume**.
- From **a one-time onboarding decision** → to **a plan they can revisit and manage later**.

## How might we

> Help a new business choose the right pricing option with confidence.

Three directions shaped the solution: **clear choice** (show Standard and optional plans as distinct, understandable routes), **visible fit** (connect expected payment volume to savings and benefits a business can judge), and **flexible control** (let merchants start now, then revisit, change, or cancel as their needs evolve).

## Iterations

We changed the amount of choice, the value frame, and the payment model to learn which explanation felt easiest to understand:

- **Four distinct offerings:** tested free limits, discounted rates, benefits, and monthly or yearly prices. The range made value visible, but made the decision heavier when volume was unclear.
- **Savings-first hierarchy:** prioritised the amount saved and made the recommended plan easier to notice. Savings sparked interest, but merchants still needed the plan to feel right for their volume.
- **Prepaid vs postpaid:** framed one route as postpaid settlement deductions and another as a prepaid plan. Payment timing turned out to be part of the decision, not just the commercial model.
- **Volume-led recommendation:** asked for expected volume, then revealed a recommendation. It helps when the volume assumption is transparent and easy to revisit.
- **0% platform-fee framing:** led with the fee a merchant could avoid. A strong hook earns attention, but boundaries must be clear enough to build trust.
- **One plan + top-ups:** collapsed the comparison into one personalised plan with included usage. Simplicity works when the future path remains visible and under the merchant's control.

We tested pricing frames in a controlled onboarding MVP: a softer savings frame (**Variant A:** standard option kept close to paid plans, modest savings labels), a stronger value contrast (**Variant B:** savings contrast and recommended option made prominent), and plan-order changes (**Variant C:** hierarchy and ordering rather than price).

Variant B was the strongest direction for plan selection, especially among registered businesses. The qualitative read mattered just as much: presentation worked (fewer plans and stronger value framing increased selection), but drop-off was mixed (pricing confusion, setup blockers, and low intent were different problems), and businesses needed to know what they gained and that a paid plan was optional.

The decision: scale the strongest pricing frame, then build the touchpoints and self-serve controls around it.

## Final solution

We made pricing an explicit step after onboarding. Before taking a first payment, every eligible business chooses a route: stay on the standard rate or select a subscription plan. **The decision is required; a paid plan is not.** The final journey: a relevant pricing route prepared from business details → an explicit choice, Standard or a plan, upfront → usage nudges, with change or cancel anytime.

<CaseImage
  src="/work/subscriptions/solution-flow.png"
  alt="Merchant journey diagram annotated with the final solution: relevant route, explicit choice, and usage nudges"
  caption="A relevant pricing route prepared from business details, an explicit choice upfront, then usage nudges with change or cancel anytime."
/>

The plan story then lives wherever a business already works, across six touchpoints, each answering a different question:

- **Home page:** a full-width banner on the highest-traffic surface, after alerts and quick actions: plan awareness from the first login, without requiring a pricing-page visit.
- **Top navigation:** a persistent Get Plus action for businesses on Standard. The upgrade stays one click away, but disappears after the business upgrades.
- **Profile dropdown:** shows the current plan and a direct management link, answering "what plan am I on?" in one click.
- **Transactions page:** a subtle single-line banner beneath the payments overview, so businesses judge better pricing against their own usage, not an abstract estimate.
- **In-product nudges:** dismissible cards after a business starts processing payments, testing social proof and value framing against real transaction intent.
- **Account & settings:** a permanent Manage Plans home combining utilization, the full plan ladder, and compare/upgrade/change/cancel controls, with no need to contact a team.

How this supports the business goal: **prompt a real decision** (every eligible business considers pricing before its first payment), **scale beyond onboarding** (stage-based touchpoints reach businesses again when plan value becomes more relevant), and **retain more value** (self-serve controls reduce manual dependency and support longer-term payment use).

In practice the solution worked across five moments: introduce the optional choice after essential payment setup; make options easy to read with simple plans, savings, limits, and a clear standard path; make the trade-off understandable without blocking the default; keep plan control visible on the dashboard (status, change, cancel, resume); and reintroduce value later, after payment activity gives the business more confidence.

The final solution had to act like a bridge, not a gate: it helped merchants compare value during onboarding and still gave them control later through dashboard plan management.

## My learnings

My role went beyond designing pricing cards. I used analytics to frame the opportunity, merchant conversations to explain the funnel, and product strategy to turn a winning variant into a journey that could scale.

- **A lift is a signal:** pair conversion movement with research before deciding why a design worked.
- **The journey is the design:** a winning screen is only useful when discovery, first use, and plan management work together.
- **Growth still needs trust:** keeping the standard rate explicit made the nudge persuasive without making payment feel mandatory.
- **Designers should know the metric:** understanding cohorts, events, and guardrails helped me shape the strategy, not only the interface.

In one line: I turned a one-rate onboarding moment into a clearer subscription journey that helps a new business understand its options, choose with confidence, and manage its plan later.
