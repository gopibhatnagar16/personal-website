## Context

Most Razorpay products are charged at standard transaction rates. One standard rate was simple, but it gave payment-ready businesses little reason to choose or deepen their use of Razorpay. The product goal: give businesses a clear route — stay on the standard rate, or choose a subscription plan they can manage later.

The subscription opportunity sits just after onboarding, when the business has given us enough context but has not yet formed a regular payment habit. The intended route: understand the business → offer a clear pricing route → help it make a first payment → support regular payment use over time. The existing journey offered one standard rate and no self-serve pricing choice, leaving committed businesses to switch gateways, contact Sales, or postpone the decision.

## Problem

After a registered business completed onboarding, the product still showed one standard rate — even though we had enough context to offer a more relevant next step. High-value merchants could request custom pricing through Contact Sales; everyone else saw 2% and nothing more.

The opportunity sat in the short window after setup: the product understood the business, but did not yet use that understanding to help it choose how to start. If pricing stayed unclear, merchants could leave before forming a transaction habit.

## Discovery

Onboarding captured useful business details, but every eligible business still saw the same rate. Context was collected, then ignored. The decision point after onboarding — when a business is ready to start but still deciding where to take its first payment — was the moment to show a clear next step: keep Standard available, and make a relevant plan easy to understand and revisit later.

To explain what the funnel couldn't, we called around **120 merchants** and had **30 meaningful conversations** across merchants who paused, stayed on the default path, and chose a paid plan. Four situations kept repeating:

- **Cautious starters** — "I am just starting out. I will upgrade later if needed."
- **Value-conscious comparers** — "The rate is better, but I do not remember the exact calculation."
- **Confused buyers** — "Do I need to pay to start accepting payments?"
- **Setup-blocked merchants** — "I was still stuck on setup, so the plan did not matter yet."

That translated directly into design requirements: make it explicit that Standard remains available, show how a plan connects to expected payment volume, and let merchants return and manage the decision later.

## How might we

> Help a new business choose the right pricing option with confidence.

Three directions shaped the solution: **clear choice** (Standard and optional plans as distinct, understandable routes), **visible fit** (connect expected payment volume to savings a business can judge), and **flexible control** (start now, then revisit, change, or cancel as needs evolve).

## Iterations

We changed the amount of choice, the value frame, and the payment model to learn which explanation felt easiest to understand:

- **Four distinct offerings** — a broader comparison made value visible, but made the decision heavier when volume was unclear.
- **Savings-first hierarchy** — savings sparked interest, but merchants still needed the plan to feel right for their volume.
- **Prepaid vs postpaid** — payment timing turned out to be part of the decision, not just the commercial model.
- **Volume-led recommendation** — a recommendation helps when the volume assumption is transparent and easy to revisit.
- **0% platform-fee framing** — a strong hook earns attention, but boundaries must be clear enough to build trust.
- **One plan + top-ups** — simplicity works when the future path remains visible and under the merchant's control.

## MVP experiment

We tested pricing frames in a controlled onboarding MVP: a softer savings frame (Variant A), a stronger value contrast (Variant B), and plan-order changes (Variant C) — across a simpler three-plan choice, savings language, volume relevance, monthly and annual billing, and recommendation states.

Variant B — the stronger contrast — was the clearest winner for plan selection, especially among registered businesses. The qualitative read mattered just as much: presentation worked, but drop-off was mixed (pricing confusion, setup blockers, and low intent were different problems), and businesses needed to know that a paid plan was optional. The whitelisted rollout also exposed a lifecycle gap: changing or cancelling a plan still required manual support.

The decision: scale the strongest pricing frame, then build the touchpoints and self-serve controls around it.

## Final solution

We made pricing an explicit step after onboarding. Before taking a first payment, every eligible business chooses a route: stay on the standard rate or select a subscription plan. **The decision is required; a paid plan is not.**

The plan story then lives wherever a business already works, across six touchpoints, each answering a different question:

- **Home page** — a full-width banner on the highest-traffic surface, for plan awareness from the first login.
- **Top navigation** — a persistent Get Plus action that disappears once the business upgrades.
- **Profile dropdown** — answers "what plan am I on?" in one click.
- **Transactions page** — a single-line banner that lets businesses judge better pricing against their own usage, not an abstract estimate.
- **In-product nudges** — dismissible cards after a business starts processing payments, testing social proof and value framing at the relevant moment.
- **Account & settings** — a permanent Manage Plans home combining utilization, the full plan ladder, and change/cancel/reactivate controls.

The final solution had to act like a bridge, not a gate: it helped merchants compare value during onboarding and still gave them control later through dashboard plan management.

## Results

**1,900 merchants chose subscriptions. Early cohorts grew payment volume by 61%.**

The clearest outcome was merchant action: more businesses selected a plan, generated recurring subscription revenue, and processed more payments with Razorpay.

## My learnings

- **A lift is a signal** — pair conversion movement with research before deciding why a design worked.
- **The journey is the design** — a winning screen is only useful when discovery, first use, and plan management work together.
- **Growth still needs trust** — keeping the standard rate explicit made the nudge persuasive without making payment feel mandatory.
- **Designers should know the metric** — understanding cohorts, events, and guardrails shaped the strategy, not only the interface.

In one line: I turned a one-rate onboarding moment into a clearer subscription journey that helps a new business understand its options, choose with confidence, and manage its plan later.
